# Bade of Theseus
*Weapon (Longsword), Legendary*

This magical weapon first appears as a plain, unremarkable shortsword that deals 1d4 Piercing damage. Though simple in form, it always feels perfectly familiar to the hand.  
When you attune to this weapon, you become cursed. This curse remains until it is ended by the Remove Curse spell or similar magic, though the wielder does not know they are cursed.

***Assimilating Edge.*** If you are attuned to the Blade of Theseus, you can attune to no more than one other weapon each week, and only if you currently have no Exhaustion levels. When you finish attuning to another sword, that attunement immediately fails. Instead, the Blade of Theseus fuses with that sword and takes on its nonmagical weapon statistics, including its damage dice, damage type, and weapon properties. The Blade of Theseus then gains an additional 1d4 damage of the same type as the absorbed sword. This bonus is cumulative and increases by 1d4 each time the Blade of Theseus absorbs a sword, to a maximum of +4d4. Each time the Blade of Theseus absorbs a sword in this way, you gain 2 Exhaustion levels.

***Continuity of the Blade.*** No matter how many swords it absorbs, the Blade of Theseus is always the same cursed item for the purpose of attunement, curses, spells, and magical effects. Its appearance changes to match the most recently absorbed sword, though close inspection reveals mismatched details from older forms: a different pommel, guard, fuller, grip, or fragment of steel.

***False Familiarity.*** While cursed, you do not perceive the Blade of Theseus as replacing other swords. To you, every sword you attempt to attune to is simply this sword in a different state: repaired, improved, restored, or altered, but never truly replaced. You cannot recognize this effect as supernatural on your own. Even if told of the curse, your mind instinctively rejects the claim unless the curse is first broken.

***Eaten Memories.*** Whenever the Blade of Theseus absorbs a sword, you forget the weapon it replaced. Your memories alter themselves to preserve the illusion of continuity. You do not remember acquiring a different sword, losing an older one, or carrying multiple distinct blades over time. Instead, you remember only that you have always had this sword, though it has changed over the years. Memories not directly tied to the lost weapon remain intact, but any story, attachment, or sentiment centered on that weapon becomes blurred, rewritten, or missing.
